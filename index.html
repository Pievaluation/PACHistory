Nebraska Partners in Prevention (NePiP)

SPF-SIG · 6 years · 2006–2012 · The originating advisory model — designed for the SPF-SIG grant structure, it did not carry forward intact into subsequent funding cycles

Origin and context

NePiP was established as part of Nebraska's Strategic Prevention Framework State Incentive Grant (SPF-SIG), funded by SAMHSA's Center for Substance Abuse Prevention. Its design closely followed CSAP's intended model for state-level advisory councils — a structure created specifically for this type of five-year infrastructure grant.

When the SPF-SIG ended, the advisory council concept carried forward into Nebraska's PFS funding as the Prevention Advisory Council (PAC) — but the institutional design changed considerably. The PAC operated under different grant requirements, a different facilitation structure, and without the executive-branch chairmanship that had characterized NePiP. The lessons from NePiP's design did not fully transfer.

SEOW context — 2016 charter, now merged with PAC

The SEOW was established in March 2007 under its own separate charter (last revised 2016). Its mission was to guide prevention planning at state and local levels by providing ongoing monitoring of epidemiological data — a data-focused, technical complement to the PAC's advisory function. The SEOW was co-chaired by DHHS leadership and included state agencies, universities, regional behavioral health authorities, and community representatives.

In PFS2023, the PAC and SEOW were formally merged into a single body, combining their previously parallel functions. The 2016 SEOW charter is now superseded by this integrated structure.

September 2025 — first planning meeting for proposed workgroup structure

The September 2025 PAC/SEOW meeting was the first planning session for a proposed two-workgroup structure. The meeting's goal was to determine high-level focus areas and begin identifying what each workgroup might work toward over approximately one year. Goals discussed are proposed, not yet finalized or underway.

Members noted local coalitions use the data in community conversations (including with youth) and to check that programming aligns with what they're seeing. Idea raised: members could host data conversations and contribute community quotes to the profile — though publicly facing documents require careful handling.

Proposed PAC workgroup

Prevention capacity workgroup

Proposed focus on building prevention infrastructure, awareness, and workforce. The group discussed three focus areas and identified possible first-year steps:

Focus areas

Public and cross-sector awareness of prevention Licensing and credentialing for prevention professionals Workforce development

Steps for the next year

1Develop a public-facing definition of substance use prevention and mental health promotion — both written and spoken
2Revise the public PAC page — update mission and vision to reflect the current structure
3Expand PAC attendance — create recruitment materials for community stakeholders across sectors

Framing ideas from the group

"Prevention changes landscapes." · "Proactive vs. reactive." · "Public health is for you — but for prevention."

Members expressed a desire to reintroduce prevention to the public — building awareness at multiple levels: city councils, county commissioners, and the general public — and to articulate the why behind prevention work rather than just describing what coalitions do.

Significance of the September 2025 planning session

The September 2025 session was the first planning meeting for a proposed workgroup structure — itself a meaningful step. The proposed goals are oriented toward tangible products (an updated EBP Matrix, a revised public PAC page, recruitment materials) rather than open-ended discussion, which aligns with what members said in November 2023 was missing from the PFS2018 workgroups.

Whether this proposed structure is formalized, and whether the workgroups sustain momentum beyond the planning stage, remains to be seen. The track record for workgroup sustainability in this PAC's history is poor — but the design intent is meaningfully different from previous attempts.

What the evidence suggests

Eight actions supported by 15+ years of evaluation data across three grant cycles

1

State the purpose at every meeting

The most persistent complaint is not knowing what the PAC is or why members are present. With high coordinator turnover, purpose must be stated at the start of every meeting — not assumed or posted in documents no one reads.

2

Maintain hybrid format permanently

Virtual meetings increased attendance 27% and broadened geographic reach. In-person meetings build the relationships virtual cannot. Both, permanently, is the evidence-supported answer.

3

Rotate in-person meeting locations

Lincoln-only meetings require rural coordinators to close their offices for a full day. Rotating locations — including western Nebraska — distributes travel equitably and signals that rural coalitions matter to the PAC.

4

Build Coalition Spotlights into every agenda

Coalition Spotlights (introduced PFS2 Year 5) delivered the peer-learning format coordinators had requested since 2014. This format works and should not be optional or intermittent.

5

Launch workgroups only with explicit deliverables

The EBP Matrix Workgroup (2019) worked because it had a product to produce. Every new workgroup needs a defined output, a deadline, and a progress report at the next PAC meeting — not just a topic and a list of members.

6

Actively recruit the missing voices

Native American, youth, immigrant/refugee, and LGBT communities have been named as absent since PFS1 Year 1. The 2025 milestone to restructure membership must be treated as a commitment, not a placeholder.

7

Remove the 13-member voting cap

The charter's "13 voting members" criterion was identified in the Year 4 charter review as a barrier to open membership. The group reached consensus that membership should be open to any interested prevention stakeholder. This revision should be formalized.

8

Insulate PAC function from staff vacancy

The PAC has been cancelled when the Prevention Administrator position is vacant (happened at least twice). Document facilitation processes, identify a backup facilitator, and designate leadership succession so one vacancy does not cancel a year's meetings.
